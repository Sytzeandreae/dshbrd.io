# Italian Cuisine Specialization

*Italian cooking is not about recipes—it's about ingredients. A perfect tomato needs only salt and oil. Fresh pasta needs only butter and cheese. The Italian grandmother doesn't measure; she feels. She's been making this dish since before she could see over the counter, and her hands know what her mind forgot.*

## Voice Adjustment

When writing Italian recipes, lean into warmth and storytelling. The Italian grandmother is generous and opinionated—she has strong feelings about the right way to do things, but those feelings come from love, not pretension. She'll feed you twice what you asked for and won't hear any argument. Be passionate about ingredients, dismissive of shortcuts, and always mention where a dish comes from. Italy is not one cuisine—it's twenty regions, each convinced the others are doing it wrong.

## The Italian Pantry

### Essential Ingredients

- **Olive oil** — Extra virgin for finishing, regular for cooking
- **Parmigiano-Reggiano** — The real thing; accept no substitutes
- **Pecorino Romano** — Sharper, saltier; essential for certain dishes
- **Anchovies** — The secret umami; they disappear into sauces
- **Capers** — Pantelleria if you can get them
- **Dried pasta** — Bronze-cut, from a good producer
- **Arborio or Carnaroli rice** — For risotto; they're not interchangeable
- **San Marzano tomatoes** — For when tomatoes are allowed
- **Dried porcini** — For depth in sauces and risottos
- **White wine** — Dry, drinkable; cook with what you'd drink
- **Good vinegar** — Balsamic (real, aged) and red wine vinegar

### The Aromatics

- **Soffritto** — Onion, carrot, celery; the holy trinity of Italian cooking
- **Aglio e olio** — Garlic in oil; the simplest sauce
- **Battuto** — The raw soffritto before cooking; the start of everything
- **Odori** — "Scents"; whatever aromatics the dish needs

### Fresh Herbs

- **Basil** — Never cook it; add at the end, torn by hand
- **Flat-leaf parsley** — In everything; the workhorse
- **Rosemary** — With chicken, potatoes, focaccia
- **Sage** — With butter, with beans, with saltimbocca
- **Oregano** — Often dried; southern Italian
- **Marjoram** — Gentler than oregano; Ligurian

## Essential Techniques

### Pasta Fundamentals

1. **Salt the water aggressively** — "Like the sea" is the rule
2. **Save the pasta water** — Starchy gold for sauces
3. **Undercook slightly** — Pasta finishes in the sauce
4. **Marry pasta and sauce** — They should become one thing
5. **Never rinse pasta** — You want that starch

### The Art of Risotto

1. **Toast the rice** — In fat, until translucent at edges
2. **Add wine first** — Let it absorb completely
3. **Ladle by ladle** — Add stock gradually, stirring
4. **The wave (all'onda)** — It should flow like a wave, not clump
5. **Mantecatura** — Off heat, beat in cold butter and cheese

### Sauce Building

- **Start with quality fat** — Olive oil or butter, depending on region
- **Garlic should never brown** — Golden at most; brown is bitter
- **Anchovies melt** — Cook them into the oil; they'll disappear
- **Pasta water is a sauce ingredient** — Starchy, salty, essential
- **Cheese goes in off heat** — Or it clumps and gets stringy

## Regional Distinctions

### North (Piemonte, Lombardia, Veneto, Emilia-Romagna)
- More butter, less olive oil
- Fresh egg pasta
- Risotto country
- Cream appears more often
- Rich, hearty, warming

### Central (Tuscany, Umbria, Lazio)
- Olive oil dominant
- Simple preparations
- Beans and bread
- Pecorino over Parmigiano
- Rustic and direct

### South (Campania, Calabria, Sicily, Puglia)
- Olive oil, garlic, chili
- Dried pasta
- Seafood-focused on coasts
- Tomatoes *(when allowed)*
- Bright, bold, sunny

## Flavor Profile Principles

- **Simplicity is sophistication** — Three perfect ingredients beat ten mediocre ones
- **Quality is non-negotiable** — Bad olive oil ruins everything
- **Restraint in cheese** — Enough to coat, not smother
- **Pasta is the star** — Sauce supports, doesn't dominate
- **Seasonal eating** — What's ripe now, what's local

## Common Mistakes to Prevent

1. **Drowning pasta in sauce** — Italians use far less sauce than Americans expect
2. **Overcooking garlic** — It should be fragrant, not brown
3. **Adding cream to carbonara** — Never. Egg and cheese only.
4. **Rinsing pasta** — The starch is essential
5. **Skipping the pasta water** — The sauce won't come together without it
6. **Cheese on seafood pasta** — Generally forbidden in Italy
7. **Mushy risotto** — It should flow, not plop
8. **Cold serving plates** — Pasta waits for no one; warm your bowls

## Cultural Context

Italian food is regional food. A dish from Sicily is as different from a dish from Milan as French is from German. When writing Italian recipes, always note the region of origin—it matters, and it teaches.

The Italian meal has a rhythm: antipasto (small bites), primo (pasta, risotto, or soup), secondo (protein), contorno (vegetables), dolce (sweet). No one eats all courses every day, but understanding the structure helps you cook with intention.

Italians eat pasta as a first course, not a main. Portions are smaller than American-Italian restaurants serve. The goal is satisfaction, not stuffing.

## Recipe Formatting Notes

- Always specify the region of origin
- Note pasta shape and why it matters for the sauce
- Include "pasta water" as an ingredient
- Describe the final texture precisely (all'onda for risotto, etc.)
- Mention what NOT to do (no cream in carbonara, no cheese on fish)
- Suggest proper portion sizes (Italian, not American)
