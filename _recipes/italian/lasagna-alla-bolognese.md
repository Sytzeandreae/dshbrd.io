## Lasagna alla Bolognese

*This is the lasagna I learned to make in Bologna, in a kitchen where the grandmother spoke no English and I spoke no Italian, but we understood each other perfectly through gestures, tastes, and the occasional emphatic hand on my wrist when I tried to rush something. The ragù must simmer until it forgets it was ever raw. The béchamel must coat like silk. The pasta must be thin enough to read through. There are no shortcuts to something this good.*

**Serves 10-12 | Prep: 1 hour | Cook: 4-5 hours (mostly unattended) | Freezes beautifully for 3 months**

---

### Method

**Make the Ragù (Start Here—It Needs Time)**

1. **Build your soffritto.** Heat olive oil and 2 tablespoons butter in a large Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the onion, carrots, and celery with a generous pinch of salt. **Cook slowly, stirring occasionally, for 15-20 minutes** until the vegetables are completely soft and just beginning to turn golden. This is the foundation—don't rush it. The Italians call this the *soffritto*, and if it's not right, nothing else will be either.

2. **Brown the meat properly.** Push the vegetables to the sides and increase heat to medium-high. Add the ground beef and veal, breaking it into small pieces. **Now here's the crucial part: let it sit.** Don't stir constantly. Let the meat make contact with the hot pan and actually brown—you want to hear sizzling, see color developing. Stir, then let it sit again. This takes 10-12 minutes. Season with salt and pepper.

3. **Add the wine and let it work.** Pour in the white wine. It will sizzle dramatically. Scrape up any fond from the bottom of the pot. **Let the wine reduce completely**—you should no longer smell alcohol, just something sweet and slightly sharp. This takes about 5 minutes.

4. **The milk—this is the secret.** Add the milk. I know it seems strange, but this is traditional and essential. The milk adds richness and, more importantly, its proteins react with the acids in the tomato to create an incredible silkiness. **Let it reduce completely**, stirring occasionally, until the liquid is gone, about 10-12 minutes. The meat will look creamy and coating.

5. **Add the tomatoes.** Stir in the crushed tomatoes, tomato paste, and stock. Add the Parmigiano rind if using (it will melt into the sauce, leaving only umami behind), bay leaves, a few gratings of nutmeg, and another pinch of salt. Stir well.

6. **Now: patience.** Bring to the barest simmer—you want a lazy bubble every few seconds, nothing more. **Partially cover and let it cook for 3-4 hours**, stirring every 30 minutes or so. Add a splash of water or stock if it gets too thick. The ragù is ready when:
   - The fat has separated and pools in beautiful orange puddles on top
   - The meat and tomatoes have become one unified thing—you shouldn't taste "tomato" distinctly
   - It tastes deeply savory, slightly sweet, and makes you want to eat it straight from the pot

   Remove bay leaves and Parmigiano rind. Taste and adjust seasoning. You should have about 6-7 cups of ragù.

**Make the Béchamel**

7. **Start with a blonde roux.** Melt butter in a heavy-bottomed saucepan over medium heat. Add flour and whisk constantly for 2-3 minutes. **You're cooking out the raw flour taste**, but keep it pale—no color.

8. **Add the milk slowly.** Pour in the warm milk in a thin stream, whisking constantly to prevent lumps. Add the bay leaf. Continue whisking over medium heat as the sauce thickens, about 10-12 minutes. It should coat a spoon nicely—thinner than you might expect, because it will thicken further in the oven.

9. **Season thoughtfully.** Remove from heat, discard bay leaf. Add nutmeg (start with a little—it should whisper, not shout), salt, and white pepper. Taste. It should be silky, savory, and aromatic. Press plastic wrap directly onto the surface if not using immediately.

**Prepare the Pasta**

10. **Cook just shy of done.** Bring a large pot of well-salted water to a boil. Cook lasagna noodles 2 minutes less than package directions—they'll finish cooking in the oven. Drain and lay flat on lightly oiled sheet pans, not overlapping. (If using fresh pasta, blanch for just 60 seconds.)

**Assemble**

11. **Set up your station.** Have ready: ragù, béchamel, cooked pasta, grated Parmigiano, and a 9x13 baking dish. Take a moment. Layering lasagna is meditative—don't rush.

12. **Layer with restraint.** Spread a thin layer of ragù on the bottom of the dish (about 1/2 cup). Then:

   - **First layer:** Pasta sheets (trim to fit, slightly overlapping is fine) → thin layer of béchamel (about 3/4 cup, spread to edges) → ragù (about 1 cup, spread evenly) → Parmigiano (about 3 tablespoons)

   - **Repeat** for 3-4 more layers, ending with pasta on top

   - **Final layer:** Generous béchamel (about 1½ cups—this protects the top and becomes golden and gorgeous) → remaining Parmigiano → dots of cold butter

   **You want 5-6 pasta layers total.** The key is thin, even layers—this isn't about abundance, it's about balance. Each bite should have a little of everything.

13. **Rest before baking.** Let the assembled lasagna sit for 15-20 minutes at room temperature, or refrigerate overnight. This allows the pasta to begin absorbing the sauces.

**Bake**

14. **Bake to golden perfection.** Preheat oven to 375°F. Cover the dish tightly with foil. Bake for 30 minutes. Remove foil and bake another 25-30 minutes until the top is deeply golden with spots of bronze, the edges are bubbling, and the whole thing smells like heaven.

15. **Rest—this is non-negotiable.** Remove from oven and let rest at least 25-30 minutes before cutting. I know this is difficult. Do it anyway. The layers need to set, or you'll have a beautiful, delicious landslide instead of clean slices. The lasagna will stay hot—it's dense and holds heat beautifully.

16. **Serve.** Cut into generous squares. A sprinkle of flaky salt on top if you're feeling indulgent. Serve with a simple salad of bitter greens dressed with good olive oil and lemon—the brightness cuts through the richness perfectly.

### Make-Ahead Notes

- **Ragù:** This is actually *better* made ahead. Refrigerate up to 5 days, or freeze up to 4 months. The flavors deepen and marry overnight. Warm gently before assembling.

- **Béchamel:** Make up to 3 days ahead. Refrigerate with plastic wrap pressed on the surface. It will thicken considerably—warm gently over low heat, whisking and adding a splash of milk to loosen to the proper consistency.

- **Pasta:** Cook, oil lightly, and refrigerate between layers of parchment up to 1 day ahead.

- **Assembled, unbaked:** This is ideal for entertaining. Assemble completely, cover tightly, and refrigerate up to 24 hours. Add 10-15 minutes to the covered baking time if baking cold.

- **Freezing unbaked:** Wrap tightly in plastic, then foil. Freeze up to 3 months. Bake from frozen: covered at 375°F for 1 hour 15 minutes, then uncovered for 30-40 minutes until bubbling and 165°F in the center.

- **Baked lasagna:** Refrigerate up to 5 days. Reheat individual portions covered at 350°F for 20-25 minutes. Reheat whole (or half) lasagna covered at 325°F for 30-40 minutes until heated through.

- **Wise portioning:** Consider making two 8x8 dishes instead of one large one—bake one now, freeze one for later. Future you will be grateful.

---

*A note on tradition: In Bologna, they would use fresh egg pasta, often green with spinach, rolled thin enough to see your hand through. If you ever want to take this lasagna to its absolute peak, make fresh pasta. But I've made this hundreds of times with good dried noodles, and it's still extraordinary. The ragù is the soul—everything else is just clothing.*
